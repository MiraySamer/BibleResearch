 1st Translation

- The first Bible translation in South America occurred in Spanish (the official language of many countries in the region) with the Spanish Bible (known as Reina-Valera Bible) in the 16th century.
- For indigenous languages, translations have been happening since the 20th century, with Quechua translations dating back to the 1960s.

- Around 400 million people in South America have access to the Bible in their native language, though a significant portion still lacks full translations in their mother tongues.

 Fun Facts:

- The Quechua Bible (spoken by millions in Peru, Ecuador & Bolivia) faced a complex journey, as the language had no written tradition before translation work began.
- Indigenous translations are incredibly important for cultural preservation, as they not only provide access to Christian teachings but also help keep the language alive in written form.
- New digital tools and audio Bibles have made it possible for many remote or isolated communities in the Amazon rainforest and Andes mountains to access the Bible.

 No Full Bibles:

- Most countries have access to at least parts of the Bible in their official languages, but indigenous languages are still underserved.
- Guarani (spoken in Paraguay and parts of Argentina & Brazil) has some Bible translations, but still needs more work.
- Tupi, Quechua & Mapudungun are other indigenous languages where Bible translation efforts are still ongoing or incomplete.

Introduction

Welcome to "The Word Across the World"

A Global Journey Through Language and Translation We are twelve girls, working together as one body with a shared passion for languages, cultures, and the power of the Bible.

Our project aims to explore how the Bible has been translated into thousands of languages across the world, connecting people from every corner of the globe through one shared message. Language is more than just a way to communicate—it’s a reflection of culture, identity, and belief. As we dive into the history of Bible translations, we uncover fascinating stories of how different cultures have embraced and adapted the Bible’s teachings in ways that speak directly to their hearts.

Through our project, we hope to highlight not only the technical aspects of translation but also the deep cultural significance that these translations hold. Whether it’s in a small village in Africa, a bustling city in Asia, or a remote island in the Pacific, the Bible has touched lives in ways that transcend language barriers. It’s a testament to the power of the Word to unite people, regardless of where they come from or what language they speak.

As we work on this project, we, the twelve girls, see ourselves as an assembly, each bringing our unique skills and perspectives, but together forming one unified body, just like the many languages that make up the worldwide community of believers. We want to showcase how language diversity is a beautiful thing and how the Bible’s message can be heard by anyone, no matter what language they speak. This website is a space for you to explore the rich history, fascinating stories, and deep significance of Bible translations.

We invite you to journey with us through the world of language and faith, discovering how a single book can connect so many different people across time and space. Thank you for joining us as we share this incredible story of the Bible’s global reach. We hope it inspires you as much as it has inspired us.

  • The Old Testament was written in Hebrew and a few parts in Aramaic (Ezra 8:4-18:6, 7:12-26, Jeremiah 11:10, Dan 4:2-28:7). Some Aramaic vocabulary was found in the books of Esther, the Song of Songs, Ezekiel, and Jonah.
  • Hebrew is the original language of the Jews, and it is attributed to Abraham the Hebrew. The Hebrew script is one of the forms of Phoenician writing, and it was written in the form of letters arranged in a row, with a hair’s length between each letter. If the line ended and the letters of the last word of this line were not completed, they would be completed in the next line. The Hebrew language is considered one of the Semitic languages, and it is written like Arabic from right to left.
  • It remained the language used until the sixth century B.C., but after the Kingdom of Judah was taken captive to Babylon by Nebuchadnezzar and the Jewish people lived in captivity for Seventy years, the language of the people of Babylon mixed with their language, resulting in the language Aramaic was spoken by the Jewish people after returning from captivity, so that when the law was read to them in Hebrew, there was someone who translated it for them in Aramaic.
  • As for the New Testament, it was written in Greek, which was more widespread, except for the Gospel of Matthew, which was written in Greek and Aramaic. Because the Old Testament had been translated in Alexandria from Hebrew to Greek in the third century BC, and was called the Septuagint, the early church received the Old Testament and collected the New Testament on it, and thus the prevailing language of the two Testaments became Greek.
